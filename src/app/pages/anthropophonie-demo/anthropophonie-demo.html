Understanding the Problem

On the UTC campus, human sounds such as conversations, footsteps, chairs scraping and doors slamming form a constant acoustic backdrop. At the university restaurant, noise levels often exceed 90 decibels, creating a stressful and exhausting atmosphere.

What if noise was not just a nuisance, but a symptom of a deeper disconnection  from both nature and others? The problem was no longer the absence of natural sounds, but the impossibility of listening.

Context

We are pleased to present our project, conducted as part of the ICX1 course at UTC CompiÃ¨gne. Launched in September 2024, this work explores the theme: "Students' relationship with nature in 2030." Over four months, our team developed reflections and innovative design directions around this topic.

The idea emerged from shared experiences within our team. For those from the countryside, the city's constant hum, especially at night when it never truly sleeps, felt overwhelming and restless. Conversely, for city dwellers, it was the rural silence and natural sounds: a rooster's crow, an owl's call that felt most disorienting, revealing just how foreign true quiet can be.

This led to a fundamental question: "What does nature mean when we live in a world saturated with human sound?"

Our ambition became clear: to use nature as a medium for collective well-being, not as decoration but as a trigger for sensory awareness.

Methodology  An Iterative Approach of Listening, Critique, and Design

Sensory Exploration: Listening Before Interpreting

We began with field experiments to understand students' auditory relationships to their environment.

  • Sound bingo: guessing animal calls to test sensitivity to natural sounds
  • Soundscape immersion: listening to recorded environments (birds, countryside mornings, markets, city sounds)
  • Semi-structured interviews: with students and staff about their daily acoustic experience

Research images

Key Findings

Familiar Spaces Evoked, Not Wilderness

Natural sounds did not bring to mind the outdoors or wilderness, but rather everyday spaces. For example, the sound of a market reminded participants of the cafeteria.

Seeking Relief from Noise, Not Nature

Students were not specifically looking for nature; they sought relief from human-made noise instead.

Acoustic Fatigue and Lack of Quiet Zones

Staff reported feeling acoustically exhausted and highlighted a lack of calm spaces for respite.

Our hypothesis was wrong  the issue wasn't bringing nature back, but restoring acoustic comfort and attention to the immediate environment. This marked a key learning: knowing when to let go of an appealing idea to stay aligned with user reality.

Through semi-structured interviews, we identified two distinct user types, which informed the creation of our personas.

  • Aurore is highly sensitive to noise and seeks quiet, calming spaces
  • Thomas is more tolerant of sound, enjoys social interaction, and unintentionally contributes to the overall noise.

Personas image

Scenarios built around these personas illustrated how the same soundscapes are experienced very differently, highlighting the need for adaptable and inclusive solutions.

To complement this qualitative insight, we conducted on-site observations and mapped the campus' soundscape, recording audio in various locations. The canteen consistently registered as the loudest area, with peaks exceeding 90 dBroughly equivalent to heavy urban trafficwhich aligned with students' experiences of noise-related discomfort.

Ideation workshop: From Individual Perception to Collective Action

We held a two-hour co-creation workshop with students in two stages:

  • Convergent Prioritization: Grounding Ideas in Reality
    Ideas were evaluated on a two-axis matrix: relevance (vertical) and complexity/originality (horizontal). Participants voted for the most feasible solution and their favorite.
  • Exploratory Ideation: Breaking Constraints
    Participants imagined an ideal "sensory break" space without budget or feasibility limits, drawing character cards with specific auditory sensitivities to inspire bold, diverse ideas.

Outcomes

Most feasible: Japanese-style water fountain with soothing sounds

Group favorite: Acoustic zoning in the canteen (quiet, moderate, lively sections)

Other ideas: Green sound-absorbing walls, furniture reconfiguration, "whisper corners"

Participants expressed confusion about the precise direction of our project. This feedback signaled the need to clarify and refocus our problem statement

Reframing: Clarifying the Core Challenge

The workshop prompted a critical reassessment of our question. Students rejected artificial nature sounds as inauthentic. We reframed the challenge to focus on user needs.

"How can we reintroduce natural sounds during lunch?"

"How can we enable students to eat in a pleasant auditory environment that balances social interaction and sound sensitivity?"

Our solution

Students highlighted that limited space makes zone separation difficult. The sound meter could induce guiltnot our goal. We want noise reduction to create calm, not stress. Following our research, we developed a two-zone solution for the university restaurant

Ground floor design

Ground Floor: The ground floor features a living green wall composed of natural sound-absorbing plants. Integrated within this vegetation are mechanical flowers that react to sound levels:

  • When noise remains at acceptable levels, the flowers stay fully bloomed and open
  • When sound exceeds the established threshold, the flowers close, returning to bud form

This creates a collective, non-intrusive feedback system that affects the entire space rather than singling out individuals.

Upper floor design

Upper Floor: The first floor is reserved (without strict enforcement) for students seeking quieter conditions. Each table features a single flower protected under a glass dome, functioning as a localized sound meter. This allows each person or group to become aware of their acoustic impact. Students are gently encouraged either to reduce their volume or to return to the ground floor if they prefer a more social atmosphere.

We selected tropical indoor plants such as Boston Fern, Helxine, and Philodendron for their dense foliage that naturally reduces sound intensity by 10-15 dB.

Concept Testing with Users

The prototype is a paper flower with a stem and petals. A ring slides along the stem, connected to the petals by paper strips. Pushing the ring down pulls the petals open; lifting it closes the flower. Though purple due to material availability, we envision a white peony symbolizing respect and affection. We brought our prototype to the university restaurant to gather feedback from users in situ.

Critical insights and iterations

Culpability effect

Central placement created a sense of surveillance

Reposition flowers on walls or overhead to diffuse attention

Engagement decay

Interest diminished after initial novelty

Introduce multiple flower types with varied reactions (e.g., by frequency range, intensity, duration)

Pedagogical ambiguity

Users weren't sure how to interpret the signal

Pair visual feedback with subtle contextual cues (color gradients, opening speed)
