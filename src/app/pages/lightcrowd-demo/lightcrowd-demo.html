Ideation & Concept Development

Design Principles

To structure our design approach, we modeled the experience around six interconnected dimensions. The material and interactive dimensions form the technical core, combining a durable, light-based bracelet with an intuitive mobile interface for quick, stress-free use. The informative and declarative dimensions define how users receive and share information, creating a simple visual language that connects individuals through light signals. Finally, the social and psychological dimensions emerge as outcomes â€” fostering empathy, reassurance, and a lasting sense of safety within the crowd.

Accessibility: usable regardless of height, mobility, or language.

Simplicity: one gesture, one meaning.

Universality: instantly understandable in any context.

Concept Testing with Users

Following our initial ideation phase, we used our journey maps to create a storyboard depicting the concert experience, now enhanced with our proposed concept. By building on the key opportunities and interactions identified in the user journeys, the storyboard allowed us to visualize how the idea could improve the experience. This scenario featuring our two personas then served as the basis for our 5-second tests, assessing users' understanding, interest, and feedback on the concept.The goal was to explore participants' understanding of the device, its perceived personal and collective value, potential limitations, and possible improvements. Overall, participants grasped the concept and found it reassuring. Some concerns did arise, however: would the light remain visible amid the chaos of a concert? Would others respond to the signal? Could the system be misused?

Aesthetic & Accessibility

Aesthetic

The visual identity draws inspiration from pop-rock concert culture, combining deep purples and luminous tones. Purple â€” a color of creativity and transition â€” evokes both artistic energy (from Monet to Hendrix) and calmness, often used in sensory environments to soothe neurodivergent audiences. LightCrowd's small luminous mascots personify this vision: anonymous, soft-edged shapes symbolizing mass collectivity, whose light reflects individual presence within the crowd â€” an echo of what the bracelet itself achieves.

Accessibility

The red signal is reserved exclusively for emergencies to minimize cognitive load. Animated light motion supports color-blind users by reinforcing meaning through movement. Tactile feedback (two distinct vibrations: activation and confirmation) ensures usability under stress or in total darkness. The bracelet design enables operation with one hand, requiring no smartphone during critical moments. For users unable to raise their arms, proximity vibrations strengthen the sense of group connection, acting as a tactile compass paired with app-based geolocation.

Design is never neutral. Every technical choice carries political and ethical weight. In trying to 'save' people, we risk surveilling, controlling, or creating new forms of exclusion

How It Works

The LightCrowd bracelet is designed for intuitive use under stress. In emergencies, a long press triggers alert mode with a red animated light, signaling urgency and mimicking the crowd parting to clear a path. Before an event, the mobile app allows users to view the venue map, locate exits, and check crowd density, ensuring safe navigation. For post-event or non-urgent situations, the group mode helps users reconnect. Each group selects a unique color during setup. If someone is separated, activating this color flashes the other members' bracelets and provides haptic feedback as a tactile guide, making it easy to reunite with the group.

Prototyping & Testing

Our testing protocol aimed to evaluate how users could intuitively reconnect with friends in a crowded, noisy context â€” such as a concert. Rather than measuring success only through performance metrics, we focused on user experience quality and perceived immersion. To capture insights systematically, we used a Rainbow Sheet to document and visualize feedback across participants.

This allowed us to:

  • Identify recurring usability issues and positive reactions.
  • Categorize findings by severity and theme (affordance, clarity, feedback, immersion).
  • Track which design hypotheses were confirmed or invalidated.

The protocol included:

  • Individual 20-minute sessions.
  • Observation of natural behavior during the simulation.
  • Post-test semi-structured interviews.
  • Ratings on ease of use, realism, and sense of connection.
