Context

While exploring this concept, several limitations emerged. The term agoraphobia proved too specific, implying clinical expertise beyond our scope and narrowing our target audience. Many people experience crowd-related anxiety without a formal diagnosis. We therefore refocused on the broader experience of anxiety in crowds. Music events â€” particularly concert pits â€” became our natural context: powerful collective spaces that many know firsthand, yet can quickly turn from excitement to discomfort or distress.

Concert experiences are often perceived as moments of euphoria and sharing. However, for some, they synonymous with anxiety. At the heart of this human sea, calls for help can be swallowed by chaos, leaving individuals with a feeling of isolation and invisibility.

Ideation & Concept Development

Design Principles

To structure our design approach, we modeled the experience around six interconnected dimensions. The material and interactive dimensions form the technical core, combining a durable, light-based bracelet with an intuitive mobile interface for quick, stress-free use. The informative and declarative dimensions define how users receive and share information, creating a simple visual language that connects individuals through light signals. Finally, the social and psychological dimensions emerge as outcomes â€” fostering empathy, reassurance, and a lasting sense of safety within the crowd.
Accessibility: usable regardless of height, mobility, or language.
Simplicity: one gesture, one meaning.
Universality: instantly understandable in any context.
